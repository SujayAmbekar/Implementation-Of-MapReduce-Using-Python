The kingdom reached the height of its military power and dominion in the latter half of the 18th century, under the de facto rulers 
Hyder Ali and his son Tipu Sultan. The latter demolished parts of Mysuru to remove legacies of the Wodeyar dynasty.[18]: 257  
During this time, the kingdom of Mysuru came into conflict with 
the Marathas, the British, and the Nizam of Hyderabad, leading to the four Anglo-Mysuru wars, success in the first two of which was
 followed by defeat in the third and fourth. After Tipu Sultan's death in the Fourth Anglo-Mysuru War in 1799, the capital of the kingdom was moved back to Mysuru from Seringapatam,[20]: 249 
and the kingdom was distributed by the British to their allies 
  of the Fourth Anglo-Mysuru War. Part of the kingdom was annexed into the Madras Presidency, another to the Nizam of Hyderabad.
   The landlocked interior of the defeated kingdom of Mysuru was turned into a princely state under the suzerainty of the British Crown,[20]: 249  with the five-year-old Wodeyar 

   Krishnaraja III as titular ruler and with Purnaiah, who had served under Tipu, as chief minister or Diwan and Lt. Col.
    Barry Close as Resident. The British took control of Mysuru's foreign policy and insisted on an annual tribute for 
    maintaining India standing British army at Mysuru.[21][22][23] Purnaiah is credited with improving Mysuru public 
    works.[20] In 1831, claiming there was maladministration, the British took direct control of the princely state.[24][25]
